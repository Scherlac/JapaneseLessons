The Bennet family lives in Longbourn, Hertfordshire. They have five daughters: Jane, Elizabeth, Mary, Kitty, and Lydia. Mrs. Bennet is desperate to find good husbands for them all. She hears that a wealthy young man has rented Netherfield Park nearby.
---
Mr. Bingley arrives at Netherfield with his friend Mr. Darcy and his sister Miss Bingley. Mrs. Bennet is delighted and insists Mr. Bennet visit Bingley immediately. The family attends a ball at Meryton. Bingley and Jane are immediately drawn to each other.
---
At the ball, Darcy seems proud and refuses to dance with anyone he does not know. He says Elizabeth is only "tolerable" and not handsome enough for him. Elizabeth hears his words and finds him disagreeable. She tells the story to her friends with laughter.
---
Jane is invited to Netherfield by Bingley's sisters. Mrs. Bennet sends her on horseback in the rain so she will have to stay overnight. Jane falls ill and must remain at Netherfield for several days. Elizabeth walks three miles through the mud to care for her sister.
---
Elizabeth stays at Netherfield while Jane recovers. Darcy finds himself admiring Elizabeth's lively mind and bright eyes. Miss Bingley flirts with Darcy but he ignores her and watches Elizabeth. He begins to realise he is developing feelings for her.
---
Mr. Collins, a pompous clergyman and cousin to Mr. Bennet, arrives at Longbourn. He will inherit Longbourn when Mr. Bennet dies. Lady Catherine de Bourgh is his patron and he speaks of her constantly. Elizabeth finds his self-importance very amusing.
---
The militia regiment arrives in Meryton. Elizabeth meets the charming and handsome Mr. Wickham. He tells her a sad story about how Darcy cheated him out of the living promised by Darcy's father. Elizabeth believes everything Wickham says and her dislike of Darcy grows stronger.
---
Bingley holds a grand ball at Netherfield. Elizabeth is surprised when Darcy asks her to dance. They argue playfully during the dance, each testing the other with sharp remarks. Darcy is more drawn to her than ever, though he would not admit it.
---
After the ball, Bingley's sisters convince him to return to London. They write to Jane suggesting Bingley has no serious intentions. Jane is quietly heartbroken but does not complain. Elizabeth is furious and suspects Darcy is responsible for keeping them apart.
---
Mr. Collins proposes marriage to Elizabeth. He is certain she will accept and cannot believe a woman would refuse such an offer. Elizabeth firmly refuses him, though he refuses to take her seriously at first. Mrs. Bennet is angry and Mr. Bennet is secretly amused.
---
Collins quickly proposes to Elizabeth's friend Charlotte Lucas. Charlotte accepts without hesitation, choosing security and a comfortable home over romantic love. Elizabeth is surprised and a little sad. But Charlotte explains that not every woman can afford to wait for love.
---
Elizabeth visits Charlotte at Hunsford in Kent, where Collins is the local vicar. They are invited to dine at Rosings Park, the grand home of Lady Catherine de Bourgh. Lady Catherine is domineering and asks Elizabeth many rude personal questions. Darcy and his cousin Colonel Fitzwilliam arrive to visit their aunt Lady Catherine.
---
Darcy visits the parsonage several times when Charlotte is out. He watches Elizabeth closely and seems restless and unsettled. Colonel Fitzwilliam mentions that Darcy recently saved a friend from an unwise marriage. Elizabeth immediately suspects this refers to Jane and Bingley and feels angry.
---
Darcy suddenly arrives alone while Elizabeth is writing a letter. He paces the room nervously. Then he declares that he loves her, though he has tried hard to fight his feelings. He mentions her inferior family connections as if they are a great obstacle he has overcome.
---
Elizabeth is astonished and then indignant. She tells Darcy she cannot accept his offer. She accuses him of destroying Jane's happiness and of treating Wickham cruelly. Darcy is shocked by her anger and leaves, wounded and stunned.
---
The next morning, Darcy delivers a long letter to Elizabeth. He explains his reasons for separating Jane and Bingley. He reveals the truth about Wickham, who ran away with Darcy's young sister Georgiana hoping to take her fortune. Wickham is a dishonest man who had already spent all of Darcy's generous gift to him.
---
Elizabeth reads the letter again and again in disbelief. Slowly she sees that she has judged both men wrongly. She was blinded by Wickham's charm and her own pride. She feels deeply ashamed of her hasty and unfair opinions of Darcy.
---
Elizabeth returns home to find Lydia has been invited to Brighton with the militia regiment. Elizabeth warns her father that Lydia is too young and too silly to go without supervision. Her father dismisses her concerns. Lydia leaves for Brighton full of excitement.
---
Elizabeth travels to Derbyshire with her aunt and uncle Gardiner. They decide to visit Pemberley, Darcy's magnificent country estate. Elizabeth is certain Darcy is not at home and agrees to visit. The housekeeper praises Darcy warmly as a kind and generous master.
---
As they tour the house, Darcy unexpectedly arrives from London. He is warm, polite, and genuinely kind to Elizabeth's aunt and uncle. He is nothing like the proud man she met before. Elizabeth sees that her first impression of him was very wrong.
---
Urgent news arrives from home. Lydia has run away from Brighton with Mr. Wickham. The family is in panic and disgrace. Elizabeth tells Darcy immediately, believing this will end any hope of a future between them.
---
The Bennet family is desperate. Lydia must marry Wickham or she is ruined, and the family name with her. Mr. Gardiner travels to London to search for them. Mr. Bennet follows, but the situation seems hopeless. Two weeks pass with no news.
---
Word arrives that Lydia and Wickham have been found and will marry. Later, Elizabeth learns from her aunt that Darcy helped find Wickham and secretly paid all his debts. He arranged the marriage entirely to help Elizabeth and her family, asking for no thanks or recognition.
---
Elizabeth is deeply moved by Darcy's kindness and sacrifice. She realises how much he has changed, or how much she has misunderstood him. She knows now that she loves him. She writes to her aunt to confirm the truth of Darcy's involvement.
---
Bingley returns to Netherfield and visits Longbourn often. Mrs. Bennet fusses over him constantly. Darcy accompanies him on some visits and greets Elizabeth quietly and warmly. Bingley clearly still loves Jane, and Jane is happy again.
---
Lady Catherine de Bourgh arrives at Longbourn in an imperious temper. She has heard a rumour that Darcy intends to marry Elizabeth. She demands that Elizabeth promise never to become engaged to Darcy. Elizabeth refuses to make any such promise.
---
Darcy hears from Lady Catherine that Elizabeth refused to give the promise. He understands this as a sign that her feelings may have changed. He visits Longbourn with Bingley. He and Elizabeth walk together and begin to speak honestly.
---
Bingley proposes to Jane and she accepts with great joy. Mrs. Bennet is ecstatic. The whole house is full of celebration and happiness. Mr. Bennet gives his blessing, very pleased with Jane's choice.
---
Darcy proposes to Elizabeth again. This time he is humble and sincere, without pride or condescension. Elizabeth accepts with happiness and tells him her feelings have changed completely. She thanks him for his letter, which opened her eyes to her own prejudice.
---
Jane and Bingley marry and settle near Longbourn. Elizabeth and Darcy marry and move to Pemberley. Both couples are happy and devoted to each other. Even Mr. Bennet, who rarely leaves his library, says that Elizabeth has made the best match in the family.
